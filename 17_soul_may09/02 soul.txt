=== SLIDE 2 ===
TITLE: Soul

CONTENT:
Urban Black popular music derived from 1950s rhythm and blues
Crystallized in late 1960s and peaked in mid-1970s
Rhythm, form, and vocal performance influenced by gospel
Lyrical subjects include romance, social relations, social and political commentary: defined by lyric content and musical features


=== SLIDE 3 ===
TITLE: Socio-Political Context

CONTENT:
Black Power Movement of mid-1960s
Student Non-Violent Coordinating Committee (SNCC) and its critical stance with respect to its parent, Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC)
Embrace of Malcolm X’s Black pride, self-determination, and self-empowerment


=== SLIDE 4 ===
TITLE: Meanings of “Soul”

CONTENT:
“Soul” becomes signifier of blackness
Soul enters public discourse after uprisings in Harlem (1964), Watts (1965), and Detroit and Newark (1967)
Identified with urban Black folk culture: music, dance, visual arts, foodways, fashion, hair styles, non-verbal communication style, and language
1969 Billboard changes from “Rhythm and Blues” to “Soul”


=== SLIDE 5 ===
TITLE: Musical Roots in Gospel

CONTENT:
Ray Charles is first to incorporate structure, harmony, rhythmic organization, and vocal style of gospel in secular music
Soul sound: singers are secular counterparts to preachers/gospel singers
Ray Charles, “This Little Girl of Mine”: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HfPL7Lb0LLM


=== SLIDE 6 ===
TITLE: Variety of Vocal Styles in Soul

CONTENT:
heterogeneous sound 
singers alternate lyrical, percussive, and raspy timbres for contrast
weaving of moans, shouts, grunts, hollers, and screams into melody
phrase endings punctuated by grunts, shouts, moans to build intensity
use of juxtapositions: vary pitches, inflections, bends, slides, melismas on passing tones (same techniques on instruments)
use of word repetition

=== SLIDE 12 ===
TITLE: Curtis Mayfield

CONTENT:
Need for change based on Chicago experiences of segregation and in the Church
Religious message of sermons, spirituals, and gospel with lyric changes
Despite positive messages, many Black-oriented and mainstream pop radio refused to play Mayfield songs
Eventually adopted by Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. and Reverend Jesse Jackson displaying the song’s relevance across integrationist and nationalist messages
